The last of the good learning games died with Carmen Sandiego and I had to find out why. PopCap games was founded in the year 2000, and we all fell in love with Bejweled, Dynomite, TyperShark, and Bookworm. Along came the first app store in 2008, and all the years since have been spent mindlessly clicking though mini-games on our phones. There some fast-math and vocabulary apps that reward memorization and quick-recall, but that is not learning.

I am nostalgic for games like The Oregon Train and The Island of Dr. Brain.

Working in the forefront of Education Research designing project-based curriculum, I found the tech was behind because we were wading through the bureaucratic channels to approve funding. High-tech developments were no longer relevant by the time the projects were approved and content was developed.

My goal was to develop tools that would reduce the burden of teachers' unpaid labor. My dissertation focused on the use of automated scoring models to score students' short answer responses. My first opportunity after school was in the EdTech industry, researching AI applications in educational contexts. After spending a LOT of time building highly generative systems trying to get LLMs to make something similar to the real thing.

AI facilitates many process very well. It can speed up content development, break down summaries, and even performs similarly to humans at classifying essays into a few scoring categories. But it is just not ready for many of the tasks to which it is being applied. AI is no match for an outlier. All of education is built on outliers. Every student has a unique set of experiences, conceptions, and misconceptions that they bring to every learning experience in their progression. Educators spend years learning to average those misconceptions into curriculum, building a years long progression that carries students through a slightly deeper understanding with each year. And AI is not ready to build a lesson backed by a years long conceptual learning progression. And generating new materials to personalize learning systems in the moment means there is no way to compare what students learned or even validate their test scores. But a lot of people around me were convinced it was working just fine at every task. I learned those people were amazing software engineers that had never developed a research backed curriculum plan.

That was when I realized there was just no education in EdTech because there were no educators. Despite knowing the shortcomings, the tech sector has laid off the educators on staff that are able to see the difference. They are ostracized for complaints when they feel materials are insufficient. The real focus of EdTech is on Fiduciary responsibility. In other words, profit above learning. Gamification, addiction, another click. It is exhausting trying to explain to an engineering team why a student learning math would have to be able to enter math into the app.

And now we have an overabundance of flashy click-to-win, pay-now-or-come-back-later, subscribe-with-friends, break this pay-wall if you want to learn the next word vocabulary games.

And then I met Dr. Gunnink. After our first discussion about the need to mix education into EdTech, I knew we were going to build something together. After knowing each other only a few weeks, I told her "We both have strong personalities, and I know we are going to fight one day. But we are going to build something amazing together one day." She did not have to ask me twice to help design the psychometric platform that will drive the soon to be first Educational game in 2 decades.

Inspired by Dr. Gunnink's tenacity and vision for Rise Evolved, I designed a system that I believe will drive the future of EdTech. The EduMetrix Ecosystem is a platform designed to connect the missing pieces in EdTech. It is the bridge between learning science and technology, with tools to facilitate learning in the classroom and integration by the dev teams.

AI has its' place and purpose, but strategic implementation means leveraging capabilities and accounting for its weaknesses.

Educators interested in putting the Ed back in EdTech can contact our team for payment structure details through the NWD Consulting Network website while we build our site and software.
